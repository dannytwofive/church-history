Church History
An Objective Journey Through the One, Holy, Catholic & Apostolic Faith
The Question This App Asks

The Christian faith was, according to its own claim, delivered once for all to the saints (Jude 1:3). It was not a system to be invented, refined over centuries, or voted into existence by councils ‚Äî but a deposit entrusted to the Apostles and handed on. The question this application attempts to investigate, as objectively as possible, is: what happened to that faith, and where can it be found today?

This is not a work of advocacy for any single tradition. It is an attempt to follow the historical evidence honestly ‚Äî to trace the figures, councils, controversies and schisms that shaped the three great streams of Christianity, and to let the sources speak for themselves. You are invited to form your own conclusions.

What You Are Looking At

Church history did not begin with a division. The early church was one ‚Äî a single fellowship of baptised believers across many cities, united by shared Scripture, shared sacraments, shared bishops in apostolic succession, and a shared rule of faith. The timeline begins there: in the Old Testament figures who prepared the way, through the Apostles, through the early Fathers who defined the faith against the first heresies.

Two ruptures divided that unity. At the Council of Chalcedon (451 CE), the Coptic, Ethiopian and other Oriental churches separated over the definition of Christ's nature ‚Äî not out of heresy, the evidence suggests, but out of a terminological misunderstanding with enormous consequences. After Chalcedon, the church continued in two streams: Coptic and Oriental on one side, the Eastern and Western church together on the other.

The second rupture ‚Äî the Great Schism of 1054 CE ‚Äî divided that remaining body into Eastern Orthodoxy and Roman Catholicism over the Filioque controversy, papal authority, and accumulated cultural distance. The timeline ends at 1204 CE, when the Fourth Crusade's sack of Constantinople made reunion practically impossible.

How to Use the Timeline
Tap any figure or event card
The detail panel opens on the right (or slides up on mobile) with a full biography, key writings, connections to other figures, and ‚Äî where relevant ‚Äî which heresies they confronted.
Read the columns
Before Chalcedon, all figures appear in a single stream. After 451 CE the timeline splits into a narrow Coptic column on the left and a wider Eastern & Western column on the right. After 1054 CE that right column splits again into Eastern Orthodox and Roman Catholic. Follow the coloured lines to track each tradition.
Tap schism banners and event strips
The purple dashed banners mark the two great schisms. Smaller event strips (councils, controversies, theological notes) appear full-width between the people ‚Äî tap any of them to read the full account.
Filter by type or search
Use the toolbar to filter by Patriarchs, Prophets, Apostles, Fathers, Popes, or Emperors. The search bar finds figures by name, dates, or biography text.
Tap connections in the detail panel
Every figure lists their connections ‚Äî theological teachers, students, opponents, contemporaries. Tapping a connection jumps directly to that figure on the timeline.
How to Use Heresies & Discernment

The Heresies tab catalogues the major doctrinal errors that the early church identified and refuted ‚Äî from Gnosticism in the first century to the Iconoclast controversy. Each entry gives the historical context, the core claim of the heresy, and the Fathers' response.

The Marks tab presents the classical marks of the true church ‚Äî Apostolicity, Eucharistic unity, Martyrdom, and others ‚Äî drawn directly from the writings of the Church Fathers. These are not invented criteria; they are the tools the early church used to distinguish itself from impostors.

The Criteria tab presents the methodological frameworks developed by Irenaeus, Ignatius of Antioch and Tertullian for identifying heresy ‚Äî the earliest systematic tools of Christian discernment.

A Note on Objectivity

No account of church history is perfectly neutral. The selection of which figures matter, which events are schisms and which are merely controversies, and how the evidence is framed ‚Äî all of these involve judgements. This application attempts to be fair to all three traditions: it does not treat Chalcedon as a straightforward Coptic defeat, nor the Great Schism as straightforwardly Rome's fault, nor the Reformers (who lie beyond this timeline's scope) as either heroes or villains.

Where the sources are disputed, this is noted. Where a figure is venerated by one tradition and condemned by another, both are acknowledged. The goal is not to tell you what to conclude ‚Äî it is to give you the best available evidence, ordered as clearly as possible, so that you can investigate for yourself.
